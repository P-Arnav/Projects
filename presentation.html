Problem Statement

The Clinical Reality

  • Chest X-ray interpretation is one of the most common yet error-prone tasks in radiology
  • Radiologists don't just look at the image — they consider patient history, age, gender, prior visits
  • Subtle findings become clearer with context: a 70-year-old smoker vs. a 25-year-old athlete
In practice, diagnosis is never image-only — context changes the interpretation.

The Gap in Current AI

  • Traditional deep learning models look only at the image
  • Patient metadata and history are completely ignored
  • Simple concatenation of features loses cross-modal interactions
Our Goal

Build a model that diagnoses 14 lung diseases from chest X-rays conditioned on patient history text — the way a real radiologist would.

Architecture Overview

The model has four main components working in concert:

1 Visual Encoder

DenseNet-121

  • Lung ROI segmentation via torchxrayvision PSPNet
  • Cropped X-ray processed by DenseNet-121
  • Produces dense visual feature maps

2 Text Encoder

Bio_ClinicalBERT

  • Pretrained on MIMIC hospital notes
  • Encodes patient history into contextual embeddings
  • Captures medical language

3 Cross-Modal Attention

Bidirectional Fusion

  • Shared 512-dim projection space
  • Image attends to text & text attends to image
  • Mutual information exchange

4 Consistency Module

Gated MLP

  • Compatibility score between modalities
  • Up/down-weights predictions on agreement
  • Resolves conflicts

Model Details

DenseNet-121 — Visual Encoder

  • 121 layers with dense block connections — each layer receives feature maps from all preceding layers
  • Prevents vanishing gradients; maximizes feature reuse across depth
  • 4 dense blocks + transition layers (avg pooling + 1×1 conv)
  • Final output: 1024-dim global average pooled feature vector
  • Projected to 512-dim shared space via linear layer
  • Pretrained on ImageNet; fine-tuned on lung ROI crops
Why DenseNet? CheXNet (Rajpurkar et al., 2017) established it as the go-to backbone for NIH CXR14 — proven strong performance on exactly this task.

Bio_ClinicalBERT — Text Encoder

  • BERT-base architecture: 12 transformer layers, 768-dim hidden state, 12 attention heads, ~110M parameters
  • Pretrained on MIMIC-III clinical notes (~880M words of real hospital EHR text)
  • Learns medical vocabulary: drug names, abbreviations like "PA view", "h/o", "yo"
  • Input format: [CLS] 67-year-old male, follow-up 3, PA view [SEP]
  • [CLS] token embedding (768-dim) used as sentence representation
  • Projected to 512-dim shared space to match visual features
Why ClinicalBERT over general BERT? General BERT has never seen "PA view" or clinical shorthand — domain pretraining gives significantly better embeddings for medical text.
Shared Projection Space

Both encoders project to the same 512-dim space before cross-modal attention — this dimensional alignment is what makes token-level interaction possible.

Key Contributions

1 History Conditioning

Unlike image-only models, we incorporate patient context as a first-class input. Age, gender, and visit information directly influence the diagnosis.

2 Bidirectional Cross-Modal Attention

Both modalities mutually inform each other at the token level — far more powerful than simple late fusion or concatenation.

3 Consistency Module

Novel gating mechanism that assesses and resolves modality agreement. When visual and textual signals conflict, the gate modulates the output.

4 Synthetic History Generation

Practical workaround enabling multimodal training on a dataset that only has images — bridging the data gap with structured metadata.
